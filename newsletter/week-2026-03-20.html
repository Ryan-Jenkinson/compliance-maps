End of Week Summary

Week of Mar 14 – 20

March 14, 2026 – March 20, 2026

A relatively steady week — no breaking rulemakings, no surprise deadlines — but the MPCA's PRISM supplemental guidance and the dual ECHA SVHC additions from February continuing to demand action make this far from a quiet period. The PRISM supplemental guide is the week's most operationally significant development: it closes ambiguity on reporting scenarios that have been sitting open since Amara's Law guidance first dropped.

Key Developments

  • MPCA published a PRISM supplemental guidance document addressing specific reporting scenarios — this is the clearest signal yet on how edge cases (multi-component products, distributor vs. manufacturer roles, partial PFAS content) will be treated. Pull it now and reconcile against your current PRISM registration queue and the reformulation candidates already identified; it may shift which SKUs need full registration vs. exemption claims. On the supply chain side, use any scenario clarifications to sharpen the language in your ongoing supplier education campaign — distributors in particular need to understand their role relative to their upstream manufacturers' registration obligations.
  • The Assent webinar on 2026 PFAS compliance is available and directly relevant — given the platform is already in use for supplier declarations, confirm whether the webinar surfaces any new PRISM data-collection workflows or supplier response templates worth integrating into the campaign.
  • Wisconsin's Senate passed multiple PFAS bills with unanimous bipartisan support, ending years of gridlock — watch whether any provisions touch product sale restrictions or supply chain disclosure, as Wisconsin suppliers in your base could face new obligations that ripple into your indirect procurement picture.
  • New Mexico's proposed rules restricting intentionally added PFAS in consumer products add another state to the patchwork — low immediate urgency given the proposal stage, but the directional signal is consistent with Safer States' broader 2026 state-action report: the multi-state product restriction landscape is expanding faster than any federal floor is materializing.
  • On EPA TRI/PFAS reporting: the concurrent signals of expansion (new substance added, broader transparency framing) and potential relief (burden-reduction proposal with possible de minimis thresholds) are in tension. The de minimis proposal is worth tracking closely — if it advances, it could affect your direct-side reporting obligations for fluoropolymer coating operations.
  • ECHA's February 4 additions of n-hexane and BPAF to the SVHC Candidate List continue to be the live REACH action items. N-hexane's Article 33 obligations are triggered now for any articles above 0.1% w/w — if your procurement team hasn't formally queried suppliers on n-hexane presence in purchased articles, that's overdue. BPAF's relevance to high-performance fluoropolymer applications means it intersects directly with your direct-side PFAS supply chain, particularly given the 3M exit and reformulation work already in progress.

Looking Ahead

The 15-week countdown to the July 1, 2026 PRISM deadline is the dominant near-term pressure point — watch for any foreign manufacturer response (or non-response) to the supplier campaign, as that's where shutdown risk is hardest to control. On the regulatory side, EPA's PFAS burden-reduction proposal moving to a comment period would be the next meaningful federal signal worth tracking.

Did You Know

N-hexane was first identified as a significant occupational neurotoxin in the 1960s following mass peripheral neuropathy cases among Italian shoe factory workers — it took roughly 60 years to make the ECHA SVHC Candidate List, which is either a testament to regulatory thoroughness or a case study in why Article 33 communication is still necessary even for "well-understood" substances.
