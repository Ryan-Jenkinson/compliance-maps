◆ AI Intelligence Summary — TSCA
The EPA is actively narrowing and reinterpreting TSCA Section 8(a)(7) PFAS reporting requirements, proposing exemptions including de minimis thresholds and articles exclusions. Simultaneously, the agency is extending postponements on TCE (trichloroethylene) and other chemical use restrictions while managing renewal of its Chemical Data Reporting and CBI claims collection processes through 2026.
Company Impact

The proposed narrowing of TSCA Section 8(a)(7) PFAS reporting—particularly exemptions for articles and de minimis uses—directly reduces your direct PFAS reporting burden for fluoropolymer coatings, BUT creates critical indirect risk: if supplier manufacturers incorrectly interpret exemptions and fail to report their PFAS-containing components to EPA, those same products may also fail Minnesota PRISM registration by July 1, 2026, cutting off your supply chain. The TCE postponement extension does not affect your immediate operations unless your manufacturing processes or suppliers use TCE in synthesis or cleaning.

Key Risks

Top risk: Supplier confusion over EPA's narrowed TSCA Section 8(a)(7) reporting scope could lead manufacturers of your sourced components (seals, gaskets, electronics, adhesives) to underreport or skip EPA TSCA reporting—and simultaneously underestimate or ignore MN PRISM registration obligations, both rooted in identical PFAS inventories. Secondary risk: the de minimis exemptions and articles exclusions under the new EPA interpretation may not align with Minnesota's broader PRISM definition, creating a compliance gap where EPA-exempt PFAS articles still require MN registration. Tertiary risk: foreign manufacturers exporting to Minnesota may not track these U.S. EPA-specific exemptions and could face surprise enforcement or supply disruption.

Next Steps

Immediately issue a targeted supplier communication clarifying that EPA's narrowed TSCA Section 8(a)(7) scope does NOT exempt PFAS-containing articles, coatings, or components from Minnesota PRISM registration—stress that MN law is independent and broader than federal exemptions. Audit your tier-1 and tier-2 suppliers' understanding of PRISM by requesting confirmation of their PFAS inventory status and whether they have engaged their upstream manufacturers (especially foreign suppliers of electronics, seals, lubricants, and adhesives) on PRISM deadlines. Cross-reference your Assent platform PFAS declarations against the finalized EPA TSCA reporting exemptions (expected mid-2026) to ensure your direct products are correctly classified under both rules. Establish a 90-day supplier verification checkpoint (target: April 1, 2026) to confirm all critical component manufacturers have committed to PRISM registration by July 1, 2026.
