◆ AI Intelligence Summary — EPR
Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR) programs are expanding across multiple US states (Maine, Oregon, California, Colorado, and emerging jurisdictions including Maryland, New Jersey, and Washington), requiring producers to fund and manage end-of-life packaging recovery and recycling. These programs shift financial and operational responsibility from municipalities to manufacturers and are creating overlapping compliance obligations with varying fee structures, timelines, and stewardship plan requirements.
Company Impact

As a manufacturer shipping products in packaging across EPR-regulated states, the company faces direct compliance costs through producer registration, stewardship plan submission, and recycled content/recyclability reporting obligations in Maine (Phase 2 fees increase July 1, 2026), Oregon, California, Colorado, Maryland (registration opens Jan 1, 2027), New Jersey, and Washington (deadline July 1, 2027). Indirectly, EPR creates supply chain friction: packaging suppliers and component manufacturers may increase costs, reduce service capacity, or deprioritize smaller customers during EPR transition periods; the company must audit supplier packaging declarations and ensure purchased components meet each state's recyclability/design standards to avoid non-compliant product shipments. Foreign suppliers exporting packaging materials or packaged goods to the US must understand these obligations or risk non-compliance penalties passed to distributors and end-buyers.

Key Risks

Missed registration and stewardship plan deadlines result in prohibition of product sales in that state and potential penalties; overlapping state definitions of 'packaging' and 'producer' create classification ambiguity and audit exposure. FTC Green Guides updates (Dec 31, 2026) on recyclability and compostability claims may invalidate current packaging marketing language and require rapid corrective action before state enforcement. Supplier delays in obtaining EPR compliance certification or submitting packaging data may disrupt component procurement, particularly for electronics and industrial items sourced through distributors who themselves face PRISM + EPR dual compliance.

Next Steps

Immediately map the company's packaging footprint by state and product line to identify which EPR programs apply (Maine Phase 2, Oregon, California, Colorado are active; Maryland, New Jersey, Washington are near-term); assign ownership for registration deadlines (Maryland Jan 2027, Washington July 2027) and stewardship plan drafts (Minnesota SF 3 July 2027). Launch targeted supplier education campaign addressing EPR + PFAS + REACH convergence: distribute state-specific packaging compliance checklists to all packaging suppliers and component manufacturers, with emphasis on recyclability attestation and foreign supplier USMCA/tariff implications. Conduct packaging design audit against FTC Green Guides (baseline Dec 2026) and state-specific recyclability standards; flag any sustainability claims requiring remediation. Integrate EPR compliance tracking into Assent platform or establish parallel EPR module to monitor supplier certifications and state filing deadlines.
